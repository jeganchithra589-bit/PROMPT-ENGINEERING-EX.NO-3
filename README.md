3.     Preceding Question Prompting:

 •	"Why is climate change a global concern? Explain how greenhouse gases contribute to global warming."
 
   - The Role of Greenhouse GasesTo understand global warming, it is helpful to look at the Greenhouse Effect.
   - While some heat retention is necessary to keep Earth habitable, human activities have increased the concentration of specific gases, trapping excess heat.
   - Solar Radiation:
      The sun sends energy to Earth in the form of light. The Earth absorbs some of this energy and radiates the rest back toward space as infrared radiation (heat).
   - The "Blanket" Effect: Greenhouse gases (GHGs) like carbon dioxide ($CO_2$), methane ($CH_4$), and nitrous oxide ($N_2O$) act like a thermal blanket.
   - They allow visible light to pass through but absorb the outgoing infrared radiation.
   - Re-radiation:
        Instead of escaping into space, these gases re-emit the heat in all directions, including back down to the Earth's surface.
     
     Global Consequences
As the concentration of these gases increases, the global average temperature rises, leading to:
Thermal Expansion: Warmer oceans take up more space, contributing to sea-level rise.
Cryosphere Melting: Glaciers and polar ice caps melt, further raising sea levels and altering ocean currents.
Feedback Loops: For example, as Arctic ice melts, the Earth's albedo (reflectivity) decreases, causing the planet to absorb even more heat.

•	"How do vaccines work? Describe the process of immunization in simple terms."

 Think of a vaccine as a "training exercise" for your immune system. Instead of waiting for a dangerous disease to strike, a vaccine introduces a safe, weakened, or inactive version of the germ to your body so your "internal security team" can learn how to fight it.
 
 The 4-Step Process of ImmunizationThe goal of immunization is to create immunity without the person actually having to get sick first. 
 --- Here is how that happens:
    - The Introduction (The "Wanted" Poster):
   The vaccine introduces an antigen—a tiny, harmless piece of a virus or bacteria (like a protein or a dead version of the germ). This acts as a "Wanted" poster for your immune system.
   - The Recognition:
          Your white blood cells (specifically B-cells and T-cells) detect this foreign invader. They realize it doesn't belong and begin building a defense strategy.
   -  The Response:
          Your body produces antibodies. These are specialized proteins designed to lock onto that specific germ and neutralize it.
   - The Memory:
          Once the "mock" infection is cleared, your body creates memory cells. These cells stay in your system for years, remembering exactly what that germ looks like and how to kill it.
